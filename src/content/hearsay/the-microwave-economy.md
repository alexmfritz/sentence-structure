There was exactly one working microwave for sixty men, and it became the most heavily regulated piece of infrastructure I have ever witnessed.

A guy named Pollard kept the list. Not a guard — Pollard. He had a legal pad and a golf pencil and a system of initials that nobody fully understood but everybody respected. You signed up after breakfast for an evening slot. Three minutes each. If you went over, the next man on the list would stand directly behind you and breathe, which was its own kind of timer.

I once watched two grown men nearly come to blows over whether reheating versus cooking counted as separate categories. Pollard ruled that it did not. The ruling held. There was no appeal, because there was no higher authority — only Pollard, the pad, and the quiet understanding that the man who controlled the microwave controlled the night.

When Pollard transferred out, he handed the legal pad to a kid who'd been inside four months. We all watched it happen like a coronation. The kid held the pad like it might bite him.

Yes, this really happened. No, I cannot explain why it worked better than most actual governments.
